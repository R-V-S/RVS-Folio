Web Design

Berklee College of Music Homepage

In 2014, Berklee was faced with an all-too-common issue amongst colleges. They had too much to say and too little space to say it on their existing homepage. As part of a theme/design overhaul, I was asked to completely replace the homepage with one that would perform better in user tests and analytics.

I formulated objectives for the homepage overhaul that were based on my own philosophy on web UX and design. Those philosophies are:

  1. Design should not constrain content. Content and navigation areas must be able to shrink and grow with no design-specific constraints.
  2. Design should convey hierarchy. Raw HTML is output in a one-dimensional, linear fashion, and this mirrors how users digest information. It's easier for users to digest information when their eyes aren't struggling to decide where on the page they should go after absorbing a piece of information.
  3. Good navigation is multi-faceted. Since different users internally categorize information in different ways, it helps to employ multiple navigational elements that frame the content in different ways.
  4. Good homepages have a hook. Just as with songs, one central hook helps to make a page memorable. This is especially true if that page has a marketing agenda.
  5. Small screen and large screen versions of a page should be as similar as possible. There is an adjustment period when a user visits a site for the first time: They have to digest the structure of the page and build their own internal model of it in their brains, which they will then use to predict where they can find content that they want. Along the lines of "The Principle of Least Astonishment," there should be no surprises when visiting the same page on a device with a different screen size.

That led to the following design decisions:

  1. Horizontal navigational elements, including the main menu, were placed into an off-canvas menu.
  2. Horizontally stacking content blocks were replaced with full-width, vertically stacking blocks.
  3. The content on the homepage was designed to direct users to high-interest content inside the site through three semi-redundant lists. Each list approaches the content from a different perspective: what the content is, who the audience is, and where Berklee offers services.
  4. An auto-playing video (with audio muted by default, but with controls to enable sound and pause/play) replaced the slider at the top of the page. The video content features Berklee students and alumni. They, and their music, have proven to be Berklee's best marketing asset.
  5. To maintain one consistent experience amongst differently sized screens, the menu was designed to remain off-canvas on the desktop and full-width elements were preferred over horizontally placed elements.
